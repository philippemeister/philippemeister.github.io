Augmented Reality Weather Training

Team: Michael Dorneich, Eliot Winer, Lori Brown, Geoff Whitehurst, Philippe Meister, Jack Miller, Kexin "Kate" Wang

Executive Summary

In general aviation, pilots encounter risky weather-related situations and are involved in accidents. The accidents mainly occur when pilots fly under visual flight rules (VFR) into instrumental meteorological conditions (IMC). The VFR-into-IMC accidents result in between 28 and 58 fatal accidents every year and monetary costs estimated at $1.64 billion in direct costs and $4.64 billion in indirect costs. We develop three-dimensional augmented reality/virtual reality (AR/VR) weather models and scenario-based activities to train student pilots to recognize and assess weather-related situations and make decisions.

Domain

General Aviation (GA) refers to all civilian flying in the United States except scheduled passenger airline service (AOPA). More than 90% of the 220,000 civil aircraft in the United States are GA aircraft. More than 80% of the 600,000 pilots in the United States fly general aviation airplanes. The professional association for these GA pilots, the Aircraft Owners and Pilots Association (AOPA), has nearly 400,000 members. In total, GA is a $20 billion a year industry that generates more than $150 billion in economic activity.

Reserach Problem

Adverse weather conditions are present in 35% of fatal GA accidents, of which 60% occur while instrument meteorological conditions are present (Fultz & Ashley, 2016). The majority of these accidents occur when GA pilots who are certified to fly under visual flight rules (VFR) fly into instrumental meteorological conditions (IMC) (Carney et al., 2015; Fultz & Ashley, 2016). The VFR-into-IMC accidents result in fatalities and monetary costs (Air Safety Institute, 2018; Fultz & Ashley, 2016). The annual number of fatal accidents ranges between 28 and 58, with even higher numbers of fatalities (Air Safety Institute, 2012, 2018). The annual estimated costs are a direct cost of $1.64 billion and an indirect cost of $4.64 billion (Sobieralski, 2013).

Preliminary Concept

The research team is currently working on training that helps student pilots practice responding to weather situations. The research team (along with others) uses three-dimensional augmented reality and/or virtual reality (AR/VR) content to enhance weather training (Dorneich et al., 2020). The work will produce 3D AR/VR thunderstorm models, which enhance how pilots acquire spatial knowledge thunderstorms, and AR/VR scenario-based activities, which provide opportunities for pilots to use their newly acquired knowledge of thunderstorms to practice recognizing and assessing weather-related situations and making weather-related decisions.
