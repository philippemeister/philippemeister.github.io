Courselink

Research and design by Philippe Meister

Problem

How do students transition from their education into a career? Some students are in professional degrees that lend themselves to this transition. But some, like humanities students, may struggle to make this transition.

Concept

Couselink helps students pepare for a career by helping them recognize and demonstrate their knowledge, skills, and abilities as they go through college.

Prototype 1

Courselink is designed in response to the literature on the developmental network approach to advising, the importance of developing a professional identity, and the role of mediation in these processes. Courselink presents a visual network interface to users that is intended to mediate student development (see Figure 1). The visual network supports students in developing network understanding of their development. The visual interface is also intended to support students in developing a professional identity.

Students use this service by inputting their learning experiences. For each learning experience, a student receives a node in their network. The student will connect each node to other related nodes in ways that form meaningful relationships between experiences. The process of using this service is like developing an eportfolio, but this process provides a different visual interface to make connections between the artifacts of work. One can imagine the network beginning with their first college experience and leading directly into a job advertisement that the student has identified as a goal for after graduation. The network, hopefully, will help them develop in ways that make it feasible for them to achieve their next goal.

User Research

Interviews with acadademic advisors, career services professionals, students, and recruiters were conducted to gain qualitative understanding of studnet developent at the university. These interviews were designed to see how they currently do their work and how Courselink could fit into their activity.

Reserach Questions

Reserach questions varied with each group of participants. The research questions for the advisors were the following:

RQ1: How do academic advisors use their software while advising students?

RQ2: How do academic advisors respond to the proposed software interface for student development (Courselink)?

Participants

Advisors and career service professionals were identified through college-level lists of academic advisors at the university. Students and recruiters were identified through a snowball sample where I contacted a few trusted reprsentatives of the group and had them recommend others in that category.

Interview Protocol

The interview protocol was developed with questions about the participants current practices in advising students as well as their reactions to a proposed interface. The interview protocol includes sections for introduction, topic-specific, technology-specific, product opportunity, product reaction, and closing. There are 1-4 interview questions within each of the sections.

Analysis

The interviews were recorded and transcribed by the author. After transcribing the interviews, I read all of the transcriptions to immerse myself in the data (Tracy, 189). Once all the transcriptions were read, I began the analytical process of identifying recurrent codes in the text (Tracy, 189). The identification of codes started to shape my view of the aspects of advising that are related to technology. I coded the transcripts in Microsoft Word and took notes about the themes that appear within each code. Directly following, I exported all the codes and notes to a separate spreadsheet. In the spreadsheet, I collected each instance of the code and began collecting related themes. At the end, I wrote results from a spreadsheet that displayed codes and recurrent themes.

Results Summary

The interviews helped me see the cracks in how students engage with career services at the university. Through transcribing and anayzing the interviews, I learned that students aren’t always prepared to make good use of career services. A reoccuring pattern is that students come in to career services in their third year when they look for internships or their fourth year when they look for jobs. Students and career centers need to spend time reflecting on the previous three years and identifying the students relevant strengths.

Prototype 2

In response to the qualitative reserach, I redesigned the prototype for studnent users. Students appear to need support in tracking their experinces through the first three years of college. Users can import courses from from their university’s learning management system. After the courses are imported, users can describe the knowledge, skills, and abilities they associate with the experience. Next, they can upload artifacts of their work. Additionally, users can add other types of experiences, such as work experiences. They system will aggregate and visualize the experince for users. After the user has input their epxerinces they can use their experince to find new experinces. They can browse job listings and compare their current knoweldge, skills, and abilites to the job listing.
